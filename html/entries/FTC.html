FIRST Tech Challenge

What is FTC?

FIRST Tech Challenge is a high school robotics competition run by the nonprofit FIRST (For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology). Every year in September, a challenge is released. The challenge compromises of tasks that your robot has to do on the 12-foot by 12-foot playing field. For example, one year you had to bring yellow stones from the depot to the other side of the board where you have to stack them. More information about FTC can be found here: https://www.firstinspires.org/robotics/ftc and more information about FIRST and its other competitions can be found here: https://www.firstinspires.org/

Team #16379 KookyBotz

I am the lead builder and programmer on team #16379 KookyBotz. Currently, we are just about to start our second season. Kickoff is on September 18th. In our rookie year, we got third (tied for second but second place had more tiebreaker points) in the robot game and made it to semifinals. Our robot disconnected in the final match, and unfortunately we lost. This year, we are trying super hard to make it to Worlds through robot game.

Rookie Year

This was the first year I had ever used an actual programming language (spare a couple of short projects) to program a robot, and it was also my first year designing robots without a kit and instructions. The challenge that year was to stack yellow stones on top of a plastic "foundation." The higher the stack you could make in 2:30 seconds (30 seconds autonomously, 2 minutes driver controlled), the more points you get. Over the course of the challenge, I made 3 major robot designs, and learned how to program robots in java. We ended the season with the best teleop as well as the best rookie robot in our interleague.

Current Year

In my rookie year, I learned how important it was to have a super good autonomous. The last summer I had spent hundreds of hours learning java, and this season I started on our robot early. While the challenge comes out in September, I started working on the robot as soon as school ended, in June. I then spent 4-5 hours every day for the next 2 months developing position tracking and path following capabilities on a drivetrain I made specifically for testing and prototyping. To follow paths, I wrote a custom implementation of the path following algorithm called PurePursuit, and to track the position I used Dead-Wheel Odometry The second part in my strategy was to learn how to use Computer Aided Design, or CAD, to design the robot before making it. This would enable me to machine custom parts and not be limited to COTS parts. After finishing the autonomous, I spent a lot of time learning how to use Solidworks CAD, and then spent a little bit of time learning how to render my designs in Fusion 360.
